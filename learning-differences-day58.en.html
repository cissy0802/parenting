Parenting & Education: Learning Differences & Teaching to the Child

Individual Differences in Learning · The Learning-Styles Myth · Spotting Strengths · Individualized Support · Not Comparing

"Teach according to the child" is an old ideal, but with our own kid it often collapses into two moves: drill whatever subject is weakest, and measure them against the neighbor's child. This chapter turns that ideal back into a few usable moves — telling real differences from labels we pin on, using a child's strength as leverage, pitching the difficulty at just-reachable, and swapping the yardstick from "other kids" back to "who they were yesterday."

01

Learning Styles: A Decades-Old Idea With Almost No Evidence

The Learning Styles Myth
Learning Science · Myth-Busting
[Core Principle]

"My kid is a visual / auditory / hands-on learner" is enormously popular — and almost entirely unsupported by evidence. Forcing instruction to fit a "style" doesn't raise achievement; worse, it can hand the child a fixed label like "I'm just not good at listening."

[Why It Doesn't Work]

Pashler and colleagues (2008, Psychological Science in the Public Interest) reviewed the field: to show "learning styles" matter, you'd need evidence that matching teaching method to a child's type raises scores — and studies meeting that bar are essentially nonexistent. Cognitive scientist Daniel Willingham puts it sharply: what dictates the best way to learn something is the content itself, not the child's "type." You look at maps, you listen to pronunciation, you get in the water to swim — the same for every child. Kids do have real differences (prior knowledge, attention span, interest), but "sensory-channel preference" isn't one of them.

[Scripts & Scenarios]

Child: "I can't remember these characters — I'm the kind of person who only learns by doing."

Don't say: "Right, you're a kinesthetic learner, so let's just skip memorizing." (a label that lets them quit)

Try: "Different things stick in different ways. This part really does lock in better if you write it out, so let's do that. But some things you just have to say over and over till they're solid — that's about the material, not your 'type.' You're not 'unable to learn it'; you just haven't practiced this one enough times yet."

[Common Traps]

① Labeling by "style" feeds a fixed mindset (see Day 2): "I'm naturally bad at listening." ② Deciding "he's visual" and then under-practicing reading, writing, or listening — skills that matter just as much. ③ Mistaking "prefers a method" for "can only learn that way." Preference and effectiveness are two different things.

[This Week's Practice + Reflection]
This week, notice whether you slap a "learner model" on your child. Swap "he's a ___ type" for a description of the situation: "this concept is clearer with a diagram." Reflection: Do you carry a label yourself — "I'm just not a math/language person"? Did it protect you, or quietly close a door for you?
02

Spotting Strengths: You Need to See the Strong Side to Face the Weak

Strength-Based Parenting
Positive Psychology · Science of Individuality
[Core Principle]

Most parents default to "drill the weakest subject," with both eyes fixed on deficits. But research suggests: identifying and investing in a child's strengths first often lifts overall confidence and intrinsic drive — including the courage to turn around and face the weak spots.

[Why It Works]

Lea Waters (University of Melbourne) finds that in "strength-based parenting" — where parents notice and affirm a child's character strengths — children report higher well-being and more resilience under stress. Mechanistically, doing what you're good at activates the brain's reward circuitry, generating an "I can do this" energy that transfers into harder domains. Harvard's Todd Rose adds the jaggedness principle: no child is uniformly balanced — everyone is a jagged profile of peaks and dips, and an "average" of a child misses both the peak and the dip that actually needs support. To be clear: a strengths lens is not abandoning weaknesses — it's using a strength as the fulcrum to move a weakness.

[Scripts & Scenarios]

Your child bombed a math test but is a wonderful, crystal-clear storyteller.

Don't say: "Math this bad — what good is anything else?" (one sentence erasing the strength)

Try: "You just explained exactly where this problem went wrong, super clearly — you're great at making complicated things make sense. Let's use that: talk me through this whole type of problem yourself. Wherever you get stuck is exactly what hasn't clicked yet." (strength becomes the tool for the weakness)

[Common Traps]

① Only hunting for strengths in "useful, score-boosting" areas — math-olympiad counts, drawing is "a waste." That's the parent's utilitarian bias, not the child's reality. ② Turning the strength into fresh pressure: "You're clearly this good at art — how'd you score so low?" ③ Swinging to the other extreme and ignoring weaknesses entirely — the basics still need a floor; a strength is not a free pass.

[This Week's Practice + Reflection]
This week, write down 3 real strengths (not just academic — kindness, persistence, curiosity, humor, caring for others all count) and find one moment to name them out loud to your child. Reflection: Which side of your child does your eye catch first — the good or the faults? Did you choose that default lens, or inherit it unnoticed from the generation before?
03

Individualized Support: Pitch It at "Just Reachable"

The Zone of Proximal Development
Developmental Psychology · Scaffolding
[Core Principle]

Operationally, "teaching to the child" is mostly calibrating difficulty: give a challenge that's just reachable with a stretch. Too easy is boring, too hard causes collapse — and the sweet spot in between is where learning actually happens.

[Why It Works]

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) is the gap between what a child can do alone and what they can do with help — and learning lives inside that gap. The parent's job is to build a scaffold: give just enough of a hint, then remove it piece by piece as the child masters the step. Help too much and you strip away the practice; help too little and you manufacture pointless frustration. The gauge is plain: all correct means it's too easy; crying-level wrong means it's too hard. The ideal is a modest error rate they can cross on their own.

[Scripts & Scenarios]

Your child is stuck on a problem, near tears: "I can't do it!"

Don't just give the answer: (that strips the practice; the lesson becomes "when stuck, wait for Mom")

Don't say "figure it out yourself": (beyond their reach right now — only frustration left)

Give the smallest hint: "Let's see which step you already know… right, step one you've got. For step two — what if I remind you to think about that formula from before?" Let them take the next step themselves. Next time, give one hint less.

[Common Traps]

① Using "other kids' pace" as the ruler instead of "where my child is right now" — the scaffold has to sit at their position. ② Over-hinting until you're quietly "doing it for them," so they never learn the independent step. ③ Parking the difficulty in the comfort zone (all correct every time) — looks smooth, but nothing new is being learned.

[This Week's Practice + Reflection]
This week pick one thing your child is learning (riding a bike, a type of problem, a passage on an instrument) and deliberately give only "just enough" hints — resist finishing it for them. Reflection: Are you more prone to "helping too much" or "pushing too hard"? Recall your own childhood — how did the adults handle that balance with you?
04

Not Comparing: Every Child Runs on Their Own Clock

The Comparison Trap
Social Comparison · Developmental Variability
[Core Principle]

"Look at so-and-so…" is probably the most common — and most wounding — habit in parenting. Developmental variation is enormous: same-age children can differ by years in reading decoding, attention, or when math "clicks." That's normal biology, not "falling behind."

[Why It Matters]

Research on social comparison shows that children measured constantly against others tend to develop performance goals (I must beat others) rather than mastery goals (I want to learn this) — and the former quit more easily at the first difficulty, because once winning is off the table, the task loses its point. The more direct harm: the shame of losing the comparison damages the safety of the parent-child bond, and the core message the child receives is "I'm not good enough." Developmental neuroscience adds that same-age brains mature at very different rates — "late bloomers" are extremely common. There's only one healthy yardstick: the child's past self — a vertical comparison.

[Scripts & Scenarios]

Your child scored below their deskmate on a test.

Don't say: "So-and-so got a 95, how'd you only get 80?" (nailing the reference point onto someone else)

Try: "This time you got two more word problems right than last time — exactly the type you drilled last week. It worked. Let's look together at the one still wrong and find where it snagged." (swap the reference point to "yesterday's you")

Comparing siblings is especially off-limits (see Day 45) — it plants hidden rivalry and rifts.

[Common Traps]

① Believing in "provocation as motivation" — assuming a comparison sparks effort, when most kids just hear "I'm not good enough." ② "Modestly" running your child down to relatives in front of them — "mine's slow, no match for yours." The child takes in every word. ③ Comparing siblings, breeding long-term covert competition.

[This Week's Practice + Reflection]
This week, cut the "look at so-and-so" phrasing completely — even just once. Replace it with "compared to last time, you…" Reflection: How did it feel when your own parents held you up against others? How do you want your child, grown, to remember your attitude toward their "progress"?

Going Deeper

1. "Teaching to the child" and "labeling the child" sound close — what's the difference?
The label is fixed and about "the person"; teaching to the child is fluid and about "this task, right now." "He's kinesthetic / he's just not a math person" pins the child to a type and implies it won't change. "This concept is clearer for him with a diagram / this kind of problem still needs more reps" describes a current state and assumes it shifts with practice. The first closes a door; the second leaves room to grow. Real individualized teaching adjusts support to where the child is now, not issues a lifetime "type certificate." Whenever you're about to say "he's a ___ type," try "right now, on ___, he needs ___" — and the label turns back into a workable observation.
2. If I never compare, won't my child lack competitive drive and lose out in the real world?
"Not comparing" isn't "not striving." Research separates two motives: beating others (performance goals) and doing the thing well (mastery goals). Over the long run, mastery goals actually withstand setbacks better and go further, because the drive comes from getting the thing done — it doesn't collapse just because you "can't win right now." What you want to give isn't the external ruler of "match whatever score others got," but the internal one of "can I be better than yesterday, can I truly learn this." A child with an internal ruler is more, not less, resilient out in the world — because they don't need to stand on someone else to confirm their own worth. Competitive drive is fine; just root it in the sturdier "competing with yourself."
3. A strengths lens is nice, but if the weakness is a core subject like math or language, you can't just leave it, right?
Of course you address it — a strengths lens is never a permit to let weaknesses rot. The difference is the posture and tools you use. First, the basics are the floor and must be held; but holding the floor doesn't mean pushing a child into hating the subject outright. Second, use the strength as a lever: a storyteller "talks" the math problem out; a hands-on kid lays out fractions with objects. Third, break the big weakness into small ZPD-sized steps (see Card 3), one reachable step at a time. Kids usually abandon a subject not because it's hard, but because inside it they've only ever experienced failure and comparison — what you can do is help them taste a little control there too.
4. I was compared my whole childhood, and "look at so-and-so" flies out the moment I'm anxious. How do I change it?
First, be gentle with yourself: that line is an automatic reflex the previous generation carved into you, not proof you don't love your child. You don't change it by white-knuckling it shut — you change it by having a ready line to say instead. Prep a replacement — "compared to last time, you…" — and rehearse it until it comes out faster than "look at so-and-so." Also notice the trigger: it usually escapes in the moment you feel anxious (afraid the child is falling behind, afraid of losing face to other parents), so the thing to work on is often your anxiety, not their score (a thread you can follow back to Day 61 on the intergenerational transmission of educational anxiety). Simply realizing "I don't want to pass this on" is already breaking the chain — awareness is the first step of change.
